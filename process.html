Secondary Research

We started by looking at existing apps and information about how older adults use technology. This helped us understand common problems they face and what kinds of features work well. It gave us a good foundation and helped us see what our app needed to do.

Target Audience

By studying how older adults use technology, we identified a need for clarity, larger text, and simple navigation. These insights shaped an experience that feels calm, supportive, and easy to use for both older adults and their carers.

  • • Higher risk of depression; many adults aged 60+ live with a mental health condition.
  • • Increased likelihood of high blood pressure and cardiovascular issues.
  • • Poor sleep can raise blood pressure and reduce overall wellbeing.
  • • Many older adults struggle to keep track of complex medication schedules.
  • • Need for large text, clear language, and simple navigation to support accessibility.
  • • Integrated mood, cognition, and reminder features can help maintain independence and wellbeing.

Testing and Iterating the Design

Heuristic Evaluation

Next, I reviewed similar apps and checked them against basic usability rules. This helped me find things that were confusing or hard to use, so we could avoid making the same mistakes in our own design.

Paper Prototyping

I then sketched simple screens on paper so I could test ideas quickly with older adults. This made it easy to tweak layouts, try different flows, and see what felt most natural before moving into high‑fidelity designs.

Paper to Pixels

Finally, we turned our paper sketches into digital designs. We focused on making everything clear, easy to read, and simple to use. This stage was about taking our earlier ideas and turning them into a polished, user‑friendly interface.
